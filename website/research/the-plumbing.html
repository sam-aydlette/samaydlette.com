Shift Left on Reciprocity

Evidence reciprocity is an architecture decision. Authorization reciprocity is a government decision. Stop waiting for the second.

Author's Note

This paper documents an implementation that runs on the infrastructure of this website. Every assertion the paper makes about the artifacts it describes is verifiable: anyone can curl the published documents from /.well-known/, run cosign verify-blob against the Sigstore bundle, browse the artifacts in the live trust dashboard, and check that what the paper claims matches what is actually being served. The implementation is open-source at github.com/sam-aydlette/samaydlette.com.

The system is small. One CSP, one operator, no end users, no federal customer data. The patterns it demonstrates are not.

I wrote earlier in Pearls, PURLs and the FedRAMP 20x Inventory Problem about why a standardized component-naming layer is what FedRAMP 20x needs to deliver on its premise. This paper picks up one layer above that. The naming layer lets a single system's reports compose. The move documented here lets a single system's control implementation compose across frameworks. The same evidence, written once, becomes seven framework views: FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate, FedRAMP 20x Class C, 800-53 Rev 4, CMMC Level 2, GovRAMP Moderate with the CJIS overlay, and TX-RAMP Levels 1 and 2. All seven are live on the trust dashboard, and their coverage figures regenerate from the published OSCAL.

One label is changing as this is written. FedRAMP announced the change in NTC-0004 (February 2026) and is formalizing it in the Consolidated Rules for 2026, expected final around the end of June 2026: a cloud service's FedRAMP standing becomes a Certification at one of Classes A through D rather than an Authorization at Low, Moderate, or High, with Class C corresponding to the former Moderate. It is an announced and in-progress relabel, not a completed one. The agency role is unchanged and remains the operative decision. An agency's Authorizing Official signs the Authorization to Operate, and that ATO is a prerequisite for the certified Marketplace listing: FedRAMP reviews the authorized package and then certifies it for government-wide reuse. Agencies reusing those ATOs remains authorization reciprocity. This paper uses Class C for the FedRAMP baseline and reserves the word authorization for the agency decision on which the certification depends.

Abstract

Federal compliance uses the term reciprocity for two distinct mechanisms. The first is a government decision: an agency's Authorizing Official accepts a risk determination and signs an Authorization to Operate, FedRAMP reviews the authorized package and certifies the service for government-wide reuse, and other agencies rely on that authorization rather than repeating the work. The second is an architecture decision: expressing one control implementation, authored once, in the shape each framework requires. The first is slow by design and cannot be accelerated by engineering. The second can be built directly, and much of the cost attributed to a lack of reciprocity is the absence of the second.

This paper documents a reference implementation of the second mechanism, moved out of the certification process and into the build. It begins with a single control hub: one 800-53 Rev 5 Component Definition describing how the system satisfies each control. Published OSCAL crosswalks and baseline profiles project that hub into seven framework views: FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate, FedRAMP 20x Class C, 800-53 Rev 4, CMMC Level 2 (NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2), GovRAMP Moderate with the CJIS overlay, and TX-RAMP Levels 1 and 2. Each additional framework requires one crosswalk or profile and one projection run; no control implementation is rewritten. The cost of supporting multiple frameworks scales with the number of crosswalks rather than with frameworks multiplied by systems.

The implementation observes one boundary: it produces evidence that composes across frameworks, not the certification a government body grants. Where a framework requires something the hub does not provide, the projection records the gap as residue. The consumer-facing component is a SCuBA-style local assessor, a tool a customer runs locally to evaluate its own configuration, reporting for each check the framework requirements that check satisfies. It records findings and does not assert an authorization.

Every claim is verifiable against the running system. The hub, the seven projections, the crosswalks, and the SCuBA output are OSCAL documents that validate against the NIST v1.2.2 schemas, and the published artifacts can be retrieved from /.well-known/ and checked with curl, jq, and cosign. The system's recurring AWS cost is on the order of $30 a year. The remaining obstacle is governance rather than engineering: whether authorization reciprocity will come to rely on evidence it did not reconcile manually.

Background: The Inventory Problem

Two FedRAMP 20x KSI signals from different CSPs nominally describe similar things, components and validations, but they do not compose into a portfolio view, because the components are named differently. CSP A identifies an EC2 instance by ARN; CSP B identifies an Azure VM by resource ID. Both are "compute instances" in any reasonable model. No automatic join exists across them.

The same fragmentation appears within a single CSP across frameworks. A 20x KSI signal and a Rev 5 SSP from the same provider describe the same system, but they are produced by different teams from different source data and rarely reconcile without manual reviewer effort. The result is what assessors call SSP drift: the security plan documents one configuration, the live system has evolved into another. Most audit findings reduce to some form of this gap.

Cross-portfolio risk aggregation is the most visible casualty. A query like "which of my authorized systems contain a particular library that just had a critical CVE" cannot be answered in any reasonable time, because the systems were inventoried by different schemas and the join across them does not exist. Each system has reasonable inventory; the inventories do not compose.

The Architectural Move: Layer Before Report

The reframe at the heart of this implementation is to treat the canonical inventory as a layer rather than a deliverable: the architectural primitive that makes everything above it composable.

The distinction matters because most compliance discussions blur it. The phrase "we need better inventories" is usually heard as "we need a better deliverable called Inventory." That framing concedes the structure of the problem to the existing fragmented model: another report, another reconciliation, another deliverable. The right framing is closer to "we need every report to reference components the same way." The inventory is what every report references, rather than a document read on its own.

The closest analog comes from networking. TCP/IP became the universal substrate of the internet by solving the addressing problem one level below where applications care, not by being the most expressive protocol. HTTP, SMTP, DNS, SSH, and every other application protocol that rides on top of TCP/IP never had to negotiate addresses with each other. They could each evolve independently, each express their own semantics, each handle their own concerns: the layer below resolved example.com to 93.184.215.14 the same way for all of them. The composability of the modern internet is a property of the addressing layer, not of any single protocol that runs on it.

The canonical inventory plays the same architectural role for compliance reports: a small set of canonical identifiers that compose without negotiation. The full identifier vocabulary is unpacked in the next section; the architectural point is that two systems naming the same thing produce bit-identical identifiers, and reports built on those identifiers join automatically.

Architecturally, this is significant: the difference between an ecosystem of reports that fragments at every framework boundary and one that composes across them. And the move requires no single party (not FedRAMP, not NIST, not CISA, not any individual CSP) to control the namespace. PURL is an open standard, content hashes are collision-resistant by construction, and cloud-resource ARNs are CSP-internal but globally unique by construction. The composability is a property of the identifiers themselves, not of any registry that issues them.

When this layer is in place, a KSI signal is no longer a "framework-specific report a CSP produces." It is "a structured document that references inventory components and reports validation results against them, in a 20x-shaped wrapper." An OSCAL Rev 5 System Security Plan is "a structured document that references inventory components and asserts control implementations against them, in a 53-shaped wrapper." An SBOM is the same again, in a CycloneDX or SPDX wrapper. A vulnerability report is the same. None of these reports needs to know about the others. They all reference the same components, and consumers join on that.

The Two Reciprocities

Reciprocity in federal compliance refers to two distinct mechanisms, separated here because they have different costs and different owners.

The first is the mechanism most discussions intend. An agency adopts a cloud service that another agency has already authorized, and reciprocity is the second agency's acceptance of the first's risk determination. This is an accountable decision made by authorizing officials, and it should remain one: a single official's risk acceptance should not automatically bind other agencies. This form of reciprocity is deliberately slow, and accelerating it is a governance problem rather than an engineering one.

The second mechanism is rarely named separately. Before any agency can accept a risk determination, the evidence must be produced in the shape the receiving framework expects. A CMMC assessor evaluates 800-171 requirements; a FedRAMP reviewer evaluates 800-53 controls; a state program uses its own baseline. The underlying system is the same in each case. Expressing one system's implementation in each framework's vocabulary is the second form of reciprocity, and it is an architecture problem.

This paper's claim is that the second form accounts for most of the cost and can be implemented without external approval, using a control hub and a set of crosswalks. The remainder of the paper describes that implementation and reports what it produces.

One Hub, Seven Spokes

The architecture applies the same principle as the naming layer beneath it: author the control evidence once, in one place, and derive every framework view from it.

The hub is a single OSCAL Component Definition. It records, exactly once, how this system meets each 800-53 Rev 5 control: the implementation narrative, and whether the control is satisfied by the system's own code, inherited from AWS, shared between the two, or genuinely out of scope. One hundred fifteen controls carry hand-written implementations, sorted into three components by who owns them. The rest fall through to family-level defaults. This is the only place in the whole system where control evidence is authored. Everything downstream is a projection of it.

A spoke is whatever a given framework needs in order to read that hub. When a framework is itself an 800-53 selection, the spoke is just a profile, a list of which controls are in scope. FedRAMP Moderate is a profile. So are GovRAMP Moderate and both TX-RAMP levels. When a framework speaks a different control language, the spoke is a crosswalk, an OSCAL Control Mapping that says which hub controls answer each foreign requirement. CMMC needs a crosswalk, because it is written in 800-171, not 800-53.

The projection is mechanical. For each control or requirement a framework names, it follows the spoke to the hub, reads the existing implementation, and records the result. The output is a framework-shaped System Security Plan, a coverage breakdown by responsibility, and a residue list of anything the framework requires that the hub does not provide. The framework-specific document is generated rather than written by hand.

Running all seven produces the coverage below, which the trust dashboard renders directly:

  • FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate, the hub's native baseline: 331 controls, fully addressed. 149 implemented by the system, 96 inherited from or shared with AWS, 86 not applicable with rationale.
  • FedRAMP 20x Class C: the deploy-time and runtime KSI signals plus the vulnerability report, held to the Class C remediation timeframes.
  • 800-53 Rev 4: projected back through the Rev 4 to Rev 5 mapping, 419 controls.
  • CMMC Level 2: 110 of 110 requirements determined, reached by chaining the 800-171 crosswalk into the Rev 4 mapping into the hub.
  • GovRAMP Moderate with the CJIS overlay: 339 of 339 controls determined.
  • TX-RAMP Level 1: 122 of 122, every control. Level 2: 322 of 322.

What matters about these numbers is the cost of producing each view. GovRAMP was added as a single profile drawn from its published controls matrix. CMMC was added as a single crosswalk drawn from Appendix D of the 800-171 publication, plus one projection run. None of the seven required changes to the hub's control implementations. This is the operational meaning of additive: the marginal cost of a framework is one configuration file and one projection run.

When a spoke first requires a control above the FedRAMP Moderate baseline, the projection records it as residue, and each such control is then dispositioned. Controls that do not apply are marked not applicable with a rationale: Voice over IP is unused, so SC-19 is not applicable, and the CJIS-specific items such as the Originating Agency Identifier do not apply because the system processes no Criminal Justice Information. Controls implemented outside the Moderate scope are recorded as implemented with their evidence: signed components, key management, separate audit storage, and resource availability cite Sigstore bundles, ACM, CloudWatch, and AWS Shield respectively. One item remained after this pass, automated cross-run vulnerability trend analysis, which was then implemented as a chart on the trust dashboard. Residue functions as a worklist: each item remains visible until it is dispositioned by applicability, by evidence, or by implementation.

CMMC and the Shared-Responsibility Question

CMMC poses a question the other frameworks do not. A FedRAMP profile asks whether the system meets a control. For a cloud service that other organizations build on, CMMC also asks what those organizations inherit. That is the Shared Responsibility Matrix, which providers typically compile by hand.

The hub answers this by projection, with one adjustment. The provider's system security plan and the customer's inheritance view are different documents, and treating one as the other is a common error. A control the provider marks not applicable because it has no end users still applies to a customer that does, where it becomes the customer's responsibility. The projection therefore reclassifies at the boundary: controls the provider implements or inherits become inherited by the customer; shared controls remain shared; controls the provider does not address become the customer's responsibility.

For this system, the customer inherits 72 of the 110 requirements in some form, 31 fully and 41 shared, and is responsible for the remaining 38. A requirement is counted as fully inherited only when every hub control behind it is fully provided; any partial dependency reduces it to shared. A figure often cited as industry shorthand for inheritance from a cloud-native platform is roughly 53 of 110 (not a standardized measure); it falls between the 31 counted as fully inherited and the 72 inherited in some form. The 72/110 is derived from the projection and can be recomputed.

One caution travels with that number. The CMMC view is reached by chaining two crosswalks (800-171 to Rev 4, Rev 4 to Rev 5), and chained crosswalks are not safely transitive: a gap or a coarse mapping in either hop can produce a confident but wrong result. This pipeline demonstrated the failure on itself. Requirement 3.12.4 (maintain a system security plan) has no entry in the 800-171 to 800-53 table, and an earlier version of the projector reported it as fully inherited over an empty set of hub controls, telling a consuming organization the provider discharged an obligation it cannot. A validation guard now rejects any requirement claimed as inherited or implemented with no backing hub control, and 3.12.4 is dispositioned explicitly as the customer's own responsibility. Composed mappings need that kind of guard; the transitivity is an assumption, not a property.

A SCuBA-Style Local Assessor: Reciprocity for the Customer

The preceding sections describe the provider side. The consumer side has a parallel structure.

The consumer tool is a SCuBA-style local assessor, modeled on CISA's ScubaGoggles and grounded in the FedRAMP 20x Secure Configuration Guidance. A customer downloads a signed policy bundle and runs it locally against its own configuration; no data is transmitted. Each check is an Open Policy Agent rule accompanied by a plain-language description. The bundle includes one policy per in-scope control, so the customer sees the full responsibility surface rather than only the controls requiring action. Most controls resolve to a default indicating that the provider satisfies them and the customer has no action; the remainder are checks the customer must pass.

The report distinguishes this from a generic scanner. Each check names the hub control it maps to, and the same crosswalks that drive the provider projections run in reverse. A single check of the customer's multi-factor configuration is annotated with every framework requirement it satisfies: 800-53 Rev 5 IA-2(1), the FedRAMP Moderate instance of it, the three Rev 4 controls it consolidated, CMMC requirement 3.5.3, the GovRAMP and CJIS entries, and both TX-RAMP levels. One configuration check thus maps to seven frameworks, computed locally from the published mappings. The output is an OSCAL Assessment Results document that validates against the NIST schema, so the customer's evidence composes in the same way the provider's does. The tool reports findings and does not assert an authorization.

Implementation

The system runs on a static website hosted on AWS: S3 for origin, CloudFront for CDN, Lambda for runtime validation, Route 53 for DNS, ACM for TLS. The compliance pipeline is a layer on top, expressed as Terraform configuration and Open Policy Agent policies. GitHub Actions drives every deploy.

The deploy chain is short. OPA evaluates the Terraform plan and every HTML artifact against the policies in infrastructure/policies.rego; violations block. Terraform applies. The deploy-time emitter (scripts/build-ksi-signal.py) joins five sources into one canonical document: Terraform state, the Lambda's package-lock, the hashed website tree, GitHub Actions provenance, and the OPA results. cosign signs that document via Sigstore keyless using the GitHub Actions OIDC identity; the certificate is issued by Fulcio and the signature is recorded in the public Rekor transparency log. Four report generators then read the canonical inventory and emit derived artifacts: the FedRAMP 20x KSI signal, the NIST OSCAL Rev 5 SSP, the NIST OSCAL POA&M, and the FedRAMP IIW (Appendix M) projection. The VDR aggregator classifies any scanner findings on the FedRAMP 20x PAIN scale and blocks the deploy if anything exceeds the Class C remediation tolerance. All artifacts plus the schema are published at /.well-known/, each Sigstore-signed.

Two CR26 rules sit alongside the deploy chain rather than inside it. Significant Change Notifications are gated at commit time: every push to main (and every PR description) is checked for an SCN-Type: tag with one of three recognized values; the default is routine-recurring per SCN-RTR-NNR and missing tags pass. The git history is the SCN audit record (SCN-CSO-MAR). The Minimum Assessment Scope rule lives in the schema: every component carries a FIPS 199 security category and an information-flow summary, populated by the deploy-time emitter (MAS-CSO-FLO). Families inherited from AWS (PE, MA, MP, parts of CP, parts of SC) cite the AWS authorization package AGENCYAMAZONEW and stop there.

A daily AWS Lambda (infrastructure/lambda/index.js) re-evaluates the live AWS configuration against the same compiled Rego the deploy gate uses, loaded as a Wasm module compiled at build time via opa build -t wasm. The Lambda is a thin host: input transformation from AWS API responses, then policy evaluation, then signal emission. There is no JavaScript port of the rules. The deploy-time gate and the runtime emitter run identical compiled policy bytes, so drift between the two compiled policies is structurally impossible. That guarantee covers the policy, not the two hand-written input adapters that shape Terraform-plan input at deploy time and AWS-API input at runtime; those adapters are the residual surface where deploy-time and runtime can still disagree. The Lambda writes a fresh runtime KSI signal at /.well-known/ksi-signal-runtime.json. That runtime signal is currently unsigned: unlike the deploy-time signal it carries no Sigstore bundle, which makes the drift detector itself the weakest link in the chain until it is signed (via a KMS key from the function, or a scheduled CI job).

The Sigstore chain replaces every key-custody question with three public-infrastructure questions. A consumer wanting to verify any signed artifact runs cosign verify-blob with the published bundle and the exact GitHub Actions workflow identity, pinned to the specific workflow file and ref. The verifier checks that the signature is valid for the bytes, that the signing certificate was issued by Fulcio to that exact identity, and that the signature appears in the public Rekor log with a valid inclusion proof. A valid signature and inclusion proof establish who signed the bytes and that the signing event is publicly logged; they do not establish that the claims inside the artifact are true. The contents are what the schema validators and the inventory gate check; the signature attests only to provenance. There is no per-CSP key to manage, no per-CSP trust relationship to establish, no rotation schedule to track, no key compromise to recover from. Trust reduces to three public services: GitHub's OIDC issuer, Fulcio's certificate authority, and Rekor's transparency log. Each has many independent observers, and a compromise is detectable by anyone running independent verification.

The recurring AWS cost is small, and at list pricing it is dominated by three always-on resources: the Route 53 hosted zone (about $6 a year), a customer-managed KMS key used for encryption at rest (about $12 a year), and two Secrets Manager secrets (about $10 a year). The serverless compute (two Lambda functions on daily and on-demand cadences, one EventBridge rule, CloudWatch logs, CloudFront, S3, and the API Gateway fronting the application) stays within the AWS free tier for this workload. Sigstore and OPA are free. The total is on the order of $30 a year, with the domain registration a separate charge of roughly $13.

The Canonical Inventory in Practice

The canonical inventory is the components[] field of the KSI signal. Each entry has a normalized type drawn from a small enum, an optional native_id (the CSP's own identifier where applicable), and a global_id block carrying one or more cross-CSP identifiers. The schema is published at /.well-known/ksi-signal.schema.json.

Five normalization decisions make the inventory composable:

Software components are identified by Package URL. PURL is global by construction. A consumer querying pkg:npm/left-pad@1.3.0 across all KSI signals from all CSPs gets exactly the systems that include it, regardless of CSP. This works because PURL is a self-describing global identifier; it would not work if each CSP minted its own software identifier.

Container images are identified by OCI digest. The sha256:<hex> form serves the same role as PURL for software libraries: a global, content-addressable identifier that does not depend on which registry hosts the image.

Cloud resources are identified by normalized type plus native ID. Cloud resources have no global identifier today. The schema does not invent a synthetic cross-CSP ID, because doing so would require either a registry (operationally expensive and politically fraught) or a hashing scheme (loses the round-trip to the CSP's own tooling). Both options are worse than the alternative. Each CSP's native shape projects into a small normalized type vocabulary, with the native_id alongside. object_store covers an S3 bucket, an Azure Blob container, a GCS bucket; cdn_distribution covers CloudFront, Azure Front Door, Cloud CDN; function covers Lambda, Azure Functions, Cloud Functions; compute_instance covers EC2, Azure VM, Compute Engine. The type is the join key for portfolio reasoning. The native ID stays specific because anyone querying the CSP's API needs it.

Static artifacts are identified by SHA-256. No PURL ecosystem exists for arbitrary static content. The bytes' hash is a perfectly good global identifier and lets consumers compare bit-equality across systems.

Hardware components are identified by HBOM reference. Per CycloneDX HBOM and CISA's 2023 Hardware Bill of Materials Framework, hardware components reference a CycloneDX HBOM document via <bom-ref>@<bom-uri>. The schema slot is reserved; this static-site implementation does not exercise it because there is no hardware to inventory.

Equally important is what the schema deliberately does not normalize: regions, account or subscription identifiers, tag schemas, IAM principal naming, and policy semantics. These vary across CSPs in ways that have no clean projection. They live in attributes, free-form, opaque to the schema. The discipline of naming what is canonical and what is not is itself a contribution. Past attempts at compliance schemas have over-reached, tried to standardize too much, and failed because the standardization cost exceeded the composability benefit. A small, defensible set of canonical names that handles the join cases is more useful than a comprehensive vocabulary that no one adopts.

Differentiation: Per-Control Status and Origination

Each implemented-requirement in the generated SSP carries differentiated values for two OSCAL properties: implementation status and control origination.

Implementation status uses the OSCAL-standard enum:

  • implemented: fully implemented by this system's code and configuration
  • partial: partially implemented, with the rest noted
  • planned: not implemented but planned (tracked in the POA&M)
  • alternative: addressed via compensating control
  • not-applicable: does not apply to this system, with rationale

Control origination uses the FedRAMP-standard enum:

  • sp-system: implemented in this system's code or configuration
  • sp-corporate: implemented at the operator/organization level (training, IR planning, review cadences)
  • shared: partially inherited from the underlying CSP, partially this system's responsibility
  • inherited: fully inherited from the underlying CSP
  • customer-configured: would be configured by an end customer (N/A here; no end customer)
  • customer-provided: would be provided by an end customer (N/A here)

The generator resolves status and origination per control through three layers:

  1. Per-control overrides for ~30 of the most load-bearing controls (CM-2/3/3.2/4/5/6/7/8, AC-2/3/4/6/17, AU-2/3/12, IA-2/3, IR-4/6/8, RA-5, SC-7/8/13, SI-2/3/4/7, SR-3/11) carry hand-written implementation statements that name specific evidence: configuration files, IAM policies, log paths, signed artifacts.
  2. Family-level fall-throughs (covering AC, AT, AU, CA, CM, CP, IA, IR, MA, MP, PE, PL, PM, PS, RA, SA, SC, SI, SR) handle the remaining controls in each family with statements that name how the family is addressed at the operator or inherited level.
  3. The *-1 (policy and procedures) controls in every family route to a templated statement pointing at the relevant documentation.

Live-signal validation results downgrade implemented to partial automatically. If a contributing KSI shows failing validations in the runtime signal, the SSP control downgrades and a remark points at the failure.

The current generator emits 331 implemented-requirements (full FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate baseline of 323 controls plus eight KSI-extension controls beyond the baseline) with this distribution: 74% implemented, 26% not-applicable, 0% partial. By origination: 52% sp-system, 19% sp-corporate, 16% shared with AWS, 13% inherited from the AWS authorization (package AGENCYAMAZONEW).

An assessor reviewing this SSP has specific things to verify per control rather than blanket statements to reconcile. The work of assessment shifts from verifying that the SSP is accurate against the live system, which is mechanical, toward verifying that the implementation statements are sufficient, which is judgment.

What This Proves

Each claim below is observable in the running system, not asserted in the abstract.

  • The schema is implementable: the live KSI signal validates against it on every deploy.
  • Sigstore keyless signing works end-to-end: cosign verify-blob succeeds against all five published bundles. No private key in the system.
  • Four reports build from one inventory on every deploy, as an observable step in the deploy pipeline.
  • Differentiated-status SSP generation: 115 hand-written hub controls, 216 family-default-generated, 331 total, all observable in the published artifact.
  • Seven framework views from one Component Definition (FedRAMP Moderate, 20x Class C, 800-53 Rev 4, CMMC Level 2, GovRAMP with CJIS, both TX-RAMP levels), each passing the NIST OSCAL schemas and fully determined after every beyond-Moderate control was dispositioned with evidence.
  • The customer SCuBA evaluates configuration locally and emits OSCAL Assessment Results that validate against the NIST schema.
  • Runtime/deploy policy parity: identical compiled Wasm bytes, drift between policy versions structurally impossible.
  • Public publication is viable at this scale: threat-modeled, bounded blast radius. For this system specifically (a single operator, no end users, no federal data, artifacts public by design), access control of compliance state is an editorial choice rather than a security requirement. This does not generalize: a system whose compliance state is sensitive would gate it, and even here the IAM policy bodies are carried only as SHA-256 hashes, conceding that some inventory content is not for publication.
  • Build-as-report for VDR: per-deploy emission, Class C SLA enforced, blocking conditions wired.
  • Recurring AWS cost on the order of $30 a year at list pricing, dominated by the Route 53 hosted zone, the customer-managed KMS key, and Secrets Manager.

Demonstrated by schema construction, gated on multi-party adoption. The implementation is a single producer. Cross-CSP join, portfolio-level aggregation, and programmatic third-party-risk consumption all rely on other parties publishing conformant signals. The schema makes those joins correct in principle; a working federation requires actual conformant signals from multiple producers. That is the chicken-and-egg pattern every coordination protocol begins with.

Related Work

The control-authoring side has prior art. OpenControl and Compliance Masonry established treating control narratives as composable source; ComplianceAsCode/SSG generates hardening content and SSP fragments from machine-readable rules; and the OSCAL project itself defines the component-definition and profile-resolution model this implementation builds on directly. What is added here is the projection step: one authored hub, read through profiles and crosswalks into many framework views, measured end to end.

The crosswalk side is where the caution lives. NIST's OLIR program publishes control-to-control mappings and is explicit that they are informational and not transitive, which is the property the CMMC section demonstrates on a live pipeline. The Secure Controls Framework and the CSA Cloud Controls Matrix are large hand-maintained crosswalks pursuing the same map-once-reuse-many goal at the catalog level rather than per system.

The provenance side draws on a maturing stack: Sigstore (Fulcio, Rekor) for keyless signing and transparency, SLSA and in-toto for build provenance, OpenVEX for exploitability assertions, GUAC for supply-chain metadata aggregation, and the CycloneDX Transparency Exchange API (TEA) and IETF SCITT for standardizing how such attestations are exchanged and audited. The canonical-inventory schema here is a small, opinionated instance of the identifier layer those efforts assume.

On the policy side, GovRAMP's verify-once-use-many reciprocity goal, the cATO literature around DoDI 8510.01, NISTIR 8011's automation of control assessment, and the FedRAMP 20x Phase One pilot (with peers such as Paramify and RegScale) all aim at continuous, machine-readable authorization. This work is one self-contained instance of that direction, distinguished by being verifiable end to end at a stable URL rather than described.

Threat Model

The assets are the published artifacts and the canonical inventory they share; a consumer relies on them to be authentic and current. The trust assumptions are explicit and external: GitHub's OIDC issuer for workflow identity, Fulcio for certificate issuance, Rekor for the public transparency log, and AWS for the runtime substrate. The signing chain removes per-system key custody but inherits the integrity of those four services.

The adversary of interest would publish a false-but-signed artifact, or silently diverge the live system from its description. The exact-identity pin defeats the first by construction: a signature from any workflow other than the named deploy workflow at the named ref fails verification, as the negative control confirms. Two residual surfaces remain, named rather than hidden. The runtime KSI signal is currently unsigned, so the drift detector is the weakest link until it carries its own bundle. And the deploy-time and runtime inputs pass through two hand-written adapters; the compiled policy is byte-identical across them, but the adapters are not, so a divergence introduced there would not be caught by policy parity alone. Evaluation

The cost of a marginal framework is measurable. Each 800-53-family spoke is a single profile: 364 lines for GovRAMP Moderate with CJIS, 147 for TX-RAMP Level 1, 347 for TX-RAMP Level 2. The one cross-language spoke, CMMC, is a 2,515-line OSCAL Control Mapping that chains through the shared 14,950-line Rev 4 to Rev 5 bridge; the beyond-Moderate dispositions add 176 lines. None of these touch the hub's 115 control implementations.

Coverage and residue are computed, not asserted. Each projection first surfaces every requirement above the FedRAMP Moderate baseline as residue (13 for GovRAMP, 25 for TX-RAMP Level 2, 2 for CMMC), then each item is dispositioned by applicability, by named evidence, or by explicit customer responsibility, leaving zero undetermined residue across all spokes while preserving the honest record of what was surfaced. The CMMC OSC-inheritance figure recomputes from the same data: 72 of 110 inherited in some form.

One measurement this evaluation does not yet include is the wall-clock time to add each framework, which is historical development data the author holds. [Operator to supply: hours to author each spoke, from first profile or crosswalk to validated coverage.]

Limitations

The caveats raised through the paper collect here. The structural-impossibility claim about drift covers the compiled policy bytes, not the two hand-written input adapters. The runtime signal is unsigned today. The access-control-is-editorial claim holds only for this system's threat model, not for systems whose compliance state is itself sensitive. The CR26 and CDS rule identifiers track an in-progress public preview, not a published rule set. Chained crosswalks are not safely transitive and require the validation guard the CMMC projector now carries. A valid signature proves provenance, not the truth of the claims it covers. And the largest limitation is structural: cross-system composition, portfolio aggregation, and third-party-risk consumption all depend on other producers publishing conformant signals, so this remains a single-producer demonstration of a pattern whose payoff is multi-party.

Alignment with the Consolidated Rules for 2026

FedRAMP’s Consolidated Rules for 2026 (CR26) reorganize the framework around machine-readable certification data, text-based equivalents in place of Word and Excel deliverables, and five rule sets that previously lived in separate processes and now fold into the default requirements. This implementation integrates all five, citing specific rule IDs the system actually mechanizes. The IDs below are taken from the CR26 public preview as of this writing; that preview is incomplete and subject to change before the final, expected around the end of June 2026, so the citations should be read as tracking a draft rather than a published rule set:

  • MAS, Minimum Assessment Scope. Every component in the canonical inventory declares a FIPS 199 security category and information flows per MAS-CSO-FLO. Third-party AWS resources are documented at the inheritance level per MAS-CSO-TPR. The schema enforces that the fields exist and are populated; the values are an editorial decision per system.
  • SCN, Significant Change Notifications. The CI workflow validates every push to main and every PR description for an SCN-Type: tag (one of adaptive, routine-recurring, transformative), with routine-recurring as the default per SCN-RTR-NNR. The git history is the audit record per SCN-CSO-MAR. Adaptive and Transformative changes carry the corresponding tier-rule notification timeframes from SCN-ADP-NTF (10 business days) and SCN-TRF-NIP / SCN-TRF-NFP / SCN-TRF-NAF (30 + 10 + 5 business days).
  • CCM, Collaborative Continuous Monitoring. Every monitoring artifact is published publicly at /.well-known/, signed via Sigstore, verifiable from anywhere. The runtime KSI emitter, deploy-time OPA gate, and build-time VDR aggregator together satisfy the persistent-reporting model in CCM-* (rules CCM-OCR-AVL, CCM-OCR-RPS, CCM-OCR-AFS, CCM-QTR-MTG). The consolidated strategy lives in docs/continuous-monitoring-plan.md.
  • VDR, Vulnerability Detection and Response. The build is the report. The aggregator classifies findings per VDR-EVA-EPA (PAIN N1-N5), VDR-EVA-EIR (internet-reachable), VDR-EVA-ELX (likely-exploitable), and the CISA KEV catalog per VDR-TFR-KEV; it blocks the deploy when any finding exceeds the Class C tolerance from VDR-TFR-PVR. Per-deploy emission satisfies VDR-RPT-PER and overshoots the monthly minimum in VDR-TFR-MHR. The VDR-RPT-VDT and VDR-RPT-AVI fields are populated for each finding and each accepted vulnerability respectively.
  • CDS, Certification Data Sharing. All certification data is published at /.well-known/ with no access gating. The KSI signal, Sigstore bundle, schema, OSCAL SSP plus bundle, OSCAL POA&M plus bundle, VDR report plus bundle, IIW CSV plus bundle, and runtime KSI signal are all served openly, consistent with CDS-CSO-PUB (publicly available), CDS-CSO-CBF (consumer-friendly format), and CDS-CSO-RPS (continuously refreshed). A consumer with curl, jq, and cosign verifies the system’s compliance state independently from anywhere on the public internet.

CR26 also retires DOCX and XLSX as acceptable formats for Class A through C certifications in favor of simple text-based equivalents (per notice NTC-0009). Every artifact in this implementation is JSON, markdown, CSV, or YAML, all text-based and diff-able. The IIW CSV is the text-based equivalent of the Excel workbook the FedRAMP processes still ask for; the OSCAL SSP carries the structured inventory the IIW projects from; the family policies, POA&M, CMP, PTA, and Rules of Behavior are markdown. CR26 also drops the diagram-and-illustration requirement after the MAS transition, so the boundary diagram in this codebase stays as human-readable context and carries no compliance weight.

Everything in this section is something CR26 will require, mechanized here ahead of the deadline rather than after it.

Business Problems This Addresses

The technical pattern, generalized, addresses several recurring business problems in cloud compliance.

Assessment cost. Most of a 3PAO assessment's cost goes to reconciling the SSP's claims against the live system's actual configuration and writing the gap into the assessment report, not to evaluating control implementations. When SSPs are auto-generated from canonical inventories, the gap is closed by construction: the SSP describes what is. An assessor's time shifts from "verifying that the SSP is accurate" to "verifying that the inventory reflects reality and the implementation statements are sufficient." The first is mechanical; the second is judgment, which is the higher-value activity. The cost of full assessment drops, and the cost of continuous assessment, which is what cATO promises and what 20x is structured around, becomes tractable instead of theoretical.

SSP drift. The most common audit finding across federal cloud authorizations is some form of "the SSP says one thing, the system does another." This finding exists because SSPs are written once, by humans, and the system changes daily. Auto-derivation eliminates the class of finding. Drift cannot exist between the SSP and the system because the SSP is generated from the system on every deploy. A consumer reading the SSP at any moment is reading the current system, not a snapshot from the last formal review.

Framework transition. CSPs in 2026 are caught mid-transition between Rev 5 and 20x, and many of them owe simultaneous compliance to state programs like GovRAMP and TX-RAMP, to CMMC for defense work, and to customer-driven assertions like SOC 2. Every one of these asks for largely the same evidence dressed in a different vocabulary. The hub-and-spoke implementation here is the direct answer, and the seven views it already produces are the proof. For a provider juggling all of these at once, the difference is between running five compliance programs and running one with five reports coming off the end of it.

Cyber insurance underwriting. The cyber insurance market has struggled to price risk because insurers cannot get reliable, current data on the systems they are insuring. They rely on questionnaires (subject to misrepresentation), point-in-time audits (immediately stale), or self-reported attestations (varying in quality). A canonical inventory with a Sigstore-signed verifiable provenance chain gives insurers something they currently cannot get: a continuously updated, cryptographically attested, machine-queryable description of the insured system's components and their security posture. Whether insurers actually adopt this is a market question, not a technical one. The technical barrier to adoption, "we cannot get reliable data," is structurally addressable.

Vendor risk management. Most third-party-risk-management functions spend the majority of their time chasing answers from suppliers. The supplier sends a SOC 2 report twelve months out of date; the TPRM team translates it into their internal scoring system; the score informs a procurement decision; and by the time the decision is made, the supplier's actual posture has changed. A supplier publishing a continuously updated KSI signal at a stable URL (even if the URL is gated behind authorized customer access) collapses the cycle. The TPRM consumer queries the signal, joins it against their portfolio, and gets an answer in seconds rather than weeks.

What Remains

The pattern is not specific to compliance. Wherever risk must be aggregated across heterogeneous systems run by independent parties, the aggregator’s work is bounded by reconciliation cost rather than analytic insight, because every reporting entity uses different identifiers, different schemas, and different attestation chains. Public-health surveillance is the sharpest example: a single outbreak signal has to be assembled from hospitals, laboratories, and jurisdictions that each name patients, specimens, and locations in their own way, so the assembling work swamps the analysis and slows the response. The same structural pattern recurs in insurance pricing, banking stability, and defense capability assessment. The canonical-inventory answer is the same in each case: do the standardization at the layer below where the differentiation actually matters.

The components for this layer already exist. PURL is well-established for software supply chain. Sigstore is operational. CycloneDX HBOM exists. SLSA provenance is gaining adoption. The piece that does not exist is the will to assemble these into a coherent compliance substrate, plus the political agreement that this is where the standardization should live.

Engineering at scale. A real IaaS CSP has tens of thousands of components in its canonical inventory. The schema scales because each component is a flat record and joins are by identifier rather than by traversal. The bottleneck is the inventory generator: a real CSP would need incremental rebuilds rather than full regeneration on every deploy. Multi-tenancy needs explicit handling: a SaaS publishing one canonical inventory for the platform plus per-tenant inventories for each customer’s configured surface is the obvious shape; the operational discipline of producing them at scale is harder. Per-customer overlays (Agency A wants FIPS 140-3, Agency B wants a GovRAMP-equivalent) are bounded engineering, each one another report generator above the same inventory layer. Publication policy is a knob; this PoC is fully public, a real regulated CSP would gate per-system signals through agency-authenticated access without changing the pipeline.

Coordination is the hard part. Cloud Service Providers compete partly on inventory opacity; uniform comparison across providers is not a thing several CSPs are eager to enable. Compliance gatekeepers earn the gap that canonical inventories would close. Regulators have sunk-cost reasons to maintain current frameworks rather than coalesce around a shared substrate. Each is a rational local incentive that pulls against the global optimum.

Coordination protocols are slow and not always solved. TCP/IP took roughly a decade from initial deployment to clear dominance. Container shipping took 30 years. They win on patient work plus lucky political openings plus early adopters who tolerate the cost of going first. They almost never win on being technically obviously correct from the beginning. This implementation is one early adopter, going first on a static personal website, demonstrating that the cost of compliance composability is on the order of $30 a year and a few weekends of engineering. Whether enough other actors follow is a question about coordination, not about the schema. The technical answer exists.
