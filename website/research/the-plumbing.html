Shift Left on Reciprocity

Evidence reciprocity is an architecture decision. Authorization reciprocity is a government decision. Stop waiting for the second to do the first.

Abstract

Federal compliance uses the word reciprocity for two distinct mechanisms with different owners and different costs. The first is a government decision: an Authorizing Official accepts a risk determination and signs an Authorization to Operate, FedRAMP certifies the package for government-wide reuse, and other agencies rely on that determination rather than repeating it. The second is an architecture decision: expressing one control implementation, authored once, in the shape each framework requires. The first is slow by design and cannot be accelerated by engineering. The second can be built directly, and much of the cost commonly attributed to a lack of reciprocity is in fact the absence of the second.

This paper presents a reference implementation of the second mechanism, moved out of the certification process and into the build pipeline. It begins from a single control hub, one 800-53 Rev 5 OSCAL Component Definition describing how the system satisfies each control, and projects that hub through published profiles and crosswalks into seven framework views: FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate, FedRAMP 20x Class C, 800-53 Rev 4, CMMC Level 2 (NIST SP 800-171 Rev 2), GovRAMP Moderate with the CJIS overlay, and TX-RAMP Levels 1 and 2. Each additional framework costs one profile or crosswalk and one projection run; no control implementation is rewritten. The cost of supporting many frameworks scales with the number of crosswalks rather than with frameworks multiplied by systems.

The implementation holds one boundary throughout: it produces evidence that composes across frameworks, never the certification a government body grants. Where a framework requires something the hub does not provide, the projection records the gap as residue and dispositions it. The consumer-facing component is a SCuBA-style local assessor that a customer runs against its own configuration, reporting for each check the framework requirements that check satisfies. Every artifact is OSCAL or another text-based format that validates against the NIST v1.2.2 schemas and can be retrieved and checked with curl, jq, and cosign. The remaining obstacle to the larger payoff is governance rather than engineering: whether authorization reciprocity will come to rely on evidence it did not reconcile by hand, and whether enough producers publish conformant signals for the cross-system joins to exist.

Availability. Every artifact described in this paper is live and independently verifiable. The signed compliance documents are served under /.well-known/ and rendered in the live trust dashboard; a reader can retrieve them with curl and jq, verify their Sigstore bundles with cosign verify-blob against the exact GitHub Actions workflow identity, and read the full source at github.com/sam-aydlette/samaydlette.com. The figures in this paper are not typed in: they are stamped at deploy time from the same OSCAL the dashboard renders, and a build gate fails if any of them drifts from its source.

Scope and disclaimer. This is a self-attested proof of concept, not a FedRAMP-certified offering, and it makes no such claim: one cloud service provider, one operator, no federal customer data, no agency sponsor, and no downstream tenants inheriting its controls. The mechanism is what generalizes; the single-site specifics are stand-ins.

Terminology. FedRAMP is relabeling a cloud service's standing from an Authorization at Low, Moderate, or High to a Certification at one of Classes A through D (NTC-0004, February 2026, finalized in the Consolidated Rules for 2026, launched 2026-06-24), with Class C positioned as adequate for most Moderate-impact uses — the rules are explicit that a Class denotes assurance depth, not an impact level. This paper uses Class C for the FedRAMP baseline and reserves authorization for the agency decision, an Authorizing Official's Authorization to Operate, on which the certification depends.

1. The Inventory Problem

Two FedRAMP 20x KSI signals from different providers nominally describe the same kinds of things, components and validation results, but they do not compose into a portfolio view, because the components are named differently. Provider A identifies an EC2 instance by ARN; Provider B identifies an Azure VM by resource ID. Both are compute instances under any reasonable model, yet no automatic join exists across them.

The same fragmentation recurs within a single provider, across frameworks. A 20x KSI signal and a Rev 5 SSP for one system are produced by different teams from different source data and rarely reconcile without manual review. The result is what assessors call SSP drift: the security plan documents one configuration and the live system has evolved into another. A large share of audit findings reduce to some form of this gap. Its most visible casualty is cross-portfolio risk aggregation. A question as basic as “which of my authorized systems contain the library that just had a critical CVE” cannot be answered quickly, because the systems were inventoried under different schemas and the join across them was never built. Each system has a reasonable inventory; the inventories do not compose.

2. Two Reciprocities

Separating the two mechanisms is the move the rest of the paper depends on.

The first is the one most discussions intend. An agency adopts a service another agency has already authorized, and reciprocity is the second agency's acceptance of the first's risk determination. This is an accountable decision made by authorizing officials and should remain one; a single official's risk acceptance should not silently bind every other agency. It is deliberately slow, and accelerating it is a governance problem, not an engineering one.

The second is rarely named on its own. Before any agency can accept a risk determination, the evidence has to exist in the shape the receiving framework expects. A CMMC assessor evaluates 800-171 requirements; a FedRAMP reviewer evaluates 800-53 controls; a state program uses its own baseline. The underlying system is identical in each case. Expressing one system's implementation in each framework's vocabulary is the second reciprocity, and it is an architecture problem. The paper's thesis is that this second form accounts for most of the cost commonly blamed on the first, and that it can be built without anyone's approval, using a control hub and a set of crosswalks.

3. The Architectural Move: Layer Before Report

The reframe at the center of the implementation is to treat the canonical inventory as a layer rather than a deliverable: the primitive that makes everything above it composable, not another document read on its own. The case for the naming layer beneath this one, where components acquire identifiers that compose, is made in a companion piece on the FedRAMP 20x inventory problem; the present paper builds the control layer one level above it.

Most compliance discussion blurs this. “We need better inventories” is usually heard as “we need a better deliverable called Inventory,” which concedes the structure of the problem to the fragmented model: another report, another reconciliation. The right reading is “we need every report to reference components the same way.” The closest analog is networking. TCP/IP became the substrate of the internet by solving addressing one level below where applications care, not by being the most expressive protocol. HTTP, SMTP, DNS, and SSH never negotiated addresses with one another; the layer below resolved example.com to an address the same way for all of them, and the composability of the internet is a property of that addressing layer rather than of any protocol riding on it.

The canonical inventory plays the same role for compliance reports: a small set of canonical identifiers that compose without negotiation, so that two systems naming the same thing emit bit-identical identifiers and reports built on them join automatically. Crucially, this requires no single party, not FedRAMP, not NIST, not CISA, not any provider, to own a namespace. PURL is an open standard, content hashes are collision-resistant by construction, and cloud ARNs are provider-internal but globally unique. Composability is a property of the identifiers, not of any registry that issues them. Once the layer is in place, a KSI signal is simply a structured document that references inventory components and reports validations against them in a 20x-shaped wrapper; an OSCAL SSP is the same documents-reference-components pattern in a 53-shaped wrapper; an SBOM is the same again in a CycloneDX or SPDX wrapper. None of these reports needs to know about the others, because they all reference the same components and consumers join on that.

4. One Hub, Seven Spokes

The control layer applies the same principle as the naming layer beneath it: author the evidence once and derive every framework view from it.

The hub is a single OSCAL Component Definition. It records, exactly once, how the system meets each 800-53 Rev 5 control: the implementation narrative, and whether the control is satisfied by the system's own code, inherited from AWS, shared between the two, or out of scope. 115 controls carry hand-written implementations, sorted into three components by who owns them; the rest fall through to family-level defaults, for 333 implemented-requirements in all. This is the only place in the system where control evidence is authored.

A spoke is whatever a framework needs in order to read that hub. When a framework is itself an 800-53 selection, the spoke is a profile, a list of in-scope controls; FedRAMP Moderate, GovRAMP Moderate, and both TX-RAMP levels are profiles. When a framework speaks a different control language, the spoke is a crosswalk, an OSCAL Control Mapping naming which hub controls answer each foreign requirement; CMMC needs one, because it is written in 800-171. The projection is mechanical: for each control or requirement a framework names, follow the spoke to the hub, read the existing implementation, and emit a framework-shaped SSP, a coverage breakdown by responsibility, and a residue list of anything the framework requires that the hub does not provide. The framework-specific document is generated, not written.

Running all seven produces the coverage the trust dashboard renders directly:

  • FedRAMP Rev 5 Moderate, the hub's native baseline: the 323-control FedRAMP Moderate baseline plus 10 KSI-extension controls beyond it, for 333/333 implemented-requirements addressed, of which 151 are implemented by the system, 96 are inherited from or shared with AWS, and 86 are not applicable with rationale.
  • FedRAMP 20x Class C: the deploy-time and runtime KSI signals plus the vulnerability report, held to the Class C remediation timeframes.
  • 800-53 Rev 4: the hub's 333 Rev 5 controls projected back through the Rev 4 to Rev 5 mapping, where they expand to a larger Rev 4 set because Rev 5 consolidated many separate Rev 4 controls into single successors.
  • CMMC Level 2: 110/110 requirements determined, reached by chaining the 800-171 crosswalk into the Rev 4 mapping into the hub.
  • GovRAMP Moderate with the CJIS overlay: 339/339 controls determined.
  • TX-RAMP Level 1: 122/122. Level 2: 322/322.

What matters is the marginal cost of each view. GovRAMP was added as a single profile drawn from its published controls matrix; CMMC as a single crosswalk from Appendix D of the 800-171 publication plus one projection run. None of the seven required a change to the hub's control implementations. That is the operational meaning of additive: a framework costs one configuration file and one run. When a spoke first names a control above the FedRAMP Moderate baseline, the projection surfaces it as residue and then dispositions it, by applicability (Voice over IP is unused, so SC-19 is not applicable; the CJIS Originating Agency Identifier does not apply with no Criminal Justice Information in scope), by named evidence (signed components, key management, and separate audit storage cite Sigstore bundles, KMS, and CloudWatch), or by implementation. Residue functions as a worklist: each item stays visible until it is dispositioned.

4.1 CMMC and the Shared-Responsibility Question

CMMC poses a question the other frameworks do not. A FedRAMP profile asks whether the system meets a control. For a service that other organizations build on, CMMC also asks what those organizations inherit, which is the Shared Responsibility Matrix that providers usually compile by hand. The hub answers this by projection, with one adjustment: the provider's SSP and the customer's inheritance view are different documents, and conflating them is a common error. A control the provider marks not applicable because it has no end users still applies to a customer that does, where it becomes the customer's responsibility. The projection therefore reclassifies at the boundary: controls the provider implements or inherits become inherited by the customer, shared controls stay shared, and controls the provider does not address become the customer's responsibility.

For this system the customer inherits 72 of the 110 requirements in some form, 31 fully and 41 shared, and is responsible for the remaining 38. A requirement counts as fully inherited only when every hub control behind it is fully provided; any partial dependency reduces it to shared. The industry shorthand of roughly 53 of 110 inherited from a cloud-native platform (not a standardized measure) falls between the 31 counted as fully inherited and the 72/110 inherited in some form; the figure here is derived from the projection and recomputes on demand.

One caution travels with that number, and the pipeline demonstrated it on itself. The CMMC view chains two crosswalks (800-171 to Rev 4, Rev 4 to Rev 5), and chained mappings are not safely transitive. Requirement 3.12.4 (maintain a system security plan) has no entry in the 800-171 to 800-53 table, and an earlier projector reported it as fully inherited over an empty set of hub controls, telling a consuming organization the provider discharged an obligation it cannot. A validation guard now rejects any requirement claimed as inherited or implemented with no backing hub control, and 3.12.4 is dispositioned explicitly as the customer's own responsibility. Composed mappings need that guard; the transitivity is an assumption, not a property.

4.2 A Customer-Side Local Assessor

The provider side has a consumer-side mirror. The consumer tool is a SCuBA-style local assessor, modeled on CISA's ScubaGoggles and grounded in the FedRAMP 20x Secure Configuration Guidance. A customer downloads a signed policy bundle and runs it locally against its own configuration; nothing is transmitted. Each check is an Open Policy Agent rule with a plain-language description, and the bundle carries one policy per in-scope control, so the customer sees the entire responsibility surface rather than only the controls that need action. Most controls resolve to a default stating that the provider satisfies them and the customer has none; the remainder are checks the customer must pass.

What distinguishes this from a generic scanner is that the same crosswalks driving the provider projections run in reverse. A single check of the customer's multi-factor configuration is annotated with every requirement it satisfies at once: 800-53 Rev 5 IA-2(1), its FedRAMP Moderate instance, the Rev 4 controls it consolidated, CMMC 3.5.3, the GovRAMP and CJIS entries, and both TX-RAMP levels. One configuration check thus maps to seven frameworks, computed locally from the published mappings. The output is an OSCAL Assessment Results document that validates against the NIST schema, so the customer's evidence composes the same way the provider's does. The tool reports findings; it does not assert an authorization.

5. The Canonical Inventory in Practice

The canonical inventory is the components[] array of the KSI signal. Each entry has a normalized type from a small enum, an optional native_id (the provider's own identifier where one applies), and a global_id block carrying one or more cross-provider identifiers. The schema is published at /.well-known/ksi-signal.schema.json. Five normalization decisions make the inventory composable:

  • Software is identified by Package URL. PURL is global by construction, so a query for pkg:npm/left-pad@1.3.0 across all signals returns exactly the systems that include it, regardless of provider. This works because PURL is self-describing; it would fail if each provider minted its own software identifier.
  • Container images are identified by OCI digest. The sha256:<hex> form is a global, content-addressable identifier that does not depend on which registry hosts the image.
  • Cloud resources are identified by normalized type plus native ID. Cloud resources have no global identifier today, and the schema does not invent a synthetic one: a registry is operationally and politically expensive, and a hashing scheme loses the round-trip to the provider's own tooling. Instead each provider's native shape projects into a small type vocabulary with the native_id alongside. object_store covers an S3 bucket, an Azure Blob container, a GCS bucket; function covers Lambda, Azure Functions, Cloud Functions; compute_instance covers EC2, Azure VM, Compute Engine. The type is the join key for portfolio reasoning; the native ID stays specific because anyone querying the provider's API needs it.
  • Static artifacts are identified by SHA-256. No PURL ecosystem exists for arbitrary static content, and the bytes' hash is a perfectly good global identifier that lets consumers compare bit-equality across systems.
  • Hardware is identified by HBOM reference. Per CycloneDX HBOM and CISA's 2023 Hardware Bill of Materials Framework, hardware components reference a CycloneDX HBOM via <bom-ref>@<bom-uri>. The slot is reserved; this static-site implementation has no hardware to inventory and does not exercise it.

Equally important is what the schema deliberately does not normalize: regions, account or subscription identifiers, tag schemas, IAM principal naming, and policy semantics, all of which vary across providers in ways that have no clean projection. They live in a free-form attributes block, opaque to the schema. Naming what is canonical and what is not is itself part of the contribution. Earlier compliance schemas over-reached, tried to standardize too much, and failed because the standardization cost exceeded the composability benefit; a small, defensible set of canonical names that handles the join cases is more useful than a comprehensive vocabulary nobody adopts.

6. Per-Control Status and Origination

Each implemented-requirement in the generated SSP carries differentiated values for two OSCAL properties rather than a blanket assertion. Implementation status uses the OSCAL-standard enum (implemented, partial, planned, alternative, not-applicable). Control origination uses the FedRAMP enum: sp-system (this system's code or configuration), sp-corporate (operator/organization level, such as training and IR planning), shared (partly inherited from the provider, partly this system), inherited (fully from the provider), and the two customer-* values that are not applicable here because there is no end customer in the boundary.

The generator resolves both per control through three layers: hand-written statements for the most load-bearing controls (CM-2/3/4/5/6/7/8, AC-2/3/4/6/17, AU-2/3/12, IA-2/3, IR-4/6/8, RA-5, SC-7/8/13, SI-2/3/4/7, SR-3/11) that name specific evidence (configuration files, IAM policies, log paths, signed artifacts); family-level fall-throughs across every 800-53 family for the remainder; and a templated statement for the -1 policy-and-procedures control in each family pointing at the relevant documentation. Live-signal results feed back into status: if a contributing KSI shows failing validations in the runtime signal, the corresponding SSP control downgrades from implemented to partial and a remark points at the failure. The effect on assessment is the point. With 115 controls carrying specific evidence and the rest resolved by named family defaults, an assessor has concrete things to verify per control rather than prose to reconcile, and the work shifts from checking that the SSP matches the system (mechanical) to judging whether the implementation statements are sufficient (judgment).

7. Implementation

The system is a static site on AWS, S3 origin, CloudFront CDN, Lambda for runtime validation, Route 53 DNS, ACM for TLS, with the compliance pipeline as a layer on top expressed in Terraform and Open Policy Agent and driven by GitHub Actions. The deploy chain is short. OPA evaluates the Terraform plan and every HTML artifact against infrastructure/policies.rego and blocks on any violation. Terraform applies. The deploy-time emitter (scripts/build-ksi-signal.py) joins five sources, Terraform state, the Lambda package-lock, the hashed website tree, GitHub Actions provenance, and the OPA results, into one canonical KSI signal, which does double duty as the inventory every other report references and as the FedRAMP 20x Class C view. Four generators then read that inventory to emit the derived reports: the OSCAL Rev 5 SSP, the OSCAL POA&M, the FedRAMP IIW (Appendix M) projection, and a vulnerability-disclosure report whose aggregator classifies any scanner findings on the FedRAMP 20x PAIN scale and blocks the deploy if anything exceeds the Class C remediation tolerance. A cross-artifact reconciliation gate then runs and fails closed before any publish, rejecting the build unless every artifact carries the same canonical signal identifier and this commit's hash and unless the live account reconciles against the inventory. Only past that gate are artifacts signed and published to /.well-known/.

Two signing paths run, and they make different trust assumptions. Deploy-time artifacts use Sigstore keyless signing. cosign obtains a short-lived Fulcio certificate bound to the GitHub Actions OIDC identity and records the signature in the public Rekor transparency log; there is no private key in the system to custody or rotate. A verifier runs cosign verify-blob with the published bundle and the exact workflow identity, pinned to the specific workflow file and ref, and checks that the signature is valid for the bytes, that the certificate was issued to that identity, and that the entry appears in Rekor with a valid inclusion proof. Every published certification-data artifact is signed this way, this paper among them (its bundle is served at /.well-known/the-plumbing.html.bundle). A valid signature establishes who signed the bytes and that the event is publicly logged; it does not establish that the claims inside are true, which is what the schema validators and the reconciliation gate are for. The runtime signal uses an asymmetric KMS key. A daily Lambda (infrastructure/lambda/index.js) re-evaluates the live AWS configuration against the same compiled Rego the deploy gate uses, signs the resulting signal with an ECDSA P-256 KMS key, and publishes the public key at /.well-known/runtime-signing-pubkey.pem so anyone can verify it. This path reintroduces a key-custody question the keyless path avoids, but the custody is AWS-managed KMS and the verification material is public.

The runtime and deploy gates run identical compiled policy bytes: the Rego is compiled to a Wasm module at build time via opa build -t wasm and both paths load it, so drift between two policy versions is structurally impossible. The Lambda is a thin host, transforming AWS API responses into policy input and emitting the signal; there is no JavaScript reimplementation of the rules. That guarantee covers the policy and not the two hand-written input adapters, one shaping Terraform-plan input at deploy time and one shaping AWS-API input at runtime, which are the residual surface where the two can still disagree (revisited in §9).

Two CR26 rules sit alongside the deploy chain. Significant Change Notifications are gated at commit time: every push to main and every PR description is checked for an SCN-Type: tag (adaptive, routine-recurring, or transformative), defaulting to routine-recurring, with the git history as the audit record. The Minimum Assessment Scope rule lives in the schema: every component carries a FIPS 199 security category and an information-flow summary, populated by the emitter, and families inherited from AWS (PE, MA, MP, and parts of CP and SC) cite the AWS FedRAMP certification package AGENCYAMAZONEW and stop there.

The recurring AWS cost is small. At list pricing it is on the order of $60 a year, dominated by four customer-managed KMS keys, one for encryption at rest, one for the DNSSEC key-signing key, one for signing the runtime signal, and one for the application's secret, at about $48 combined. The Route 53 hosted zone (about $6) and a single Secrets Manager secret (about $5) make up most of the remainder. The serverless components, two Lambda functions, an EventBridge rule, CloudWatch Logs, CloudFront, S3, and the API Gateway fronting the application, stay within the free tier for this workload, and Sigstore and OPA are free. Domain registration is a separate charge of roughly $13 a year.

8. Evaluation

The marginal cost of a framework is measurable in the artifacts themselves. Each 800-53-family spoke is a single profile, 364 lines for GovRAMP Moderate with CJIS, 147 for TX-RAMP Level 1, 347 for TX-RAMP Level 2. The one cross-language spoke, CMMC, is a 2,515-line OSCAL Control Mapping that chains through the shared 14,950-line Rev 4 to Rev 5 bridge, with 176 lines of beyond-Moderate dispositions. None of these touch the hub's 115 hand-written control implementations. (This measures artifact size, not authoring wall-clock, which was not instrumented and is left to future work.)

Coverage and residue are computed rather than asserted. Each projection first surfaces every requirement above the FedRAMP Moderate baseline as residue (13 for GovRAMP, 25 for TX-RAMP Level 2, 2 for CMMC), then dispositions each by applicability, named evidence, or explicit customer responsibility, leaving zero undetermined residue across all spokes while preserving the honest record of what was surfaced. The CMMC inheritance figure recomputes from the same data: 72 of 110 inherited in some form.

What the running system demonstrates, each point observable rather than asserted:

  • The schema is implementable: the live KSI signal validates against it on every deploy.
  • Keyless signing works end to end: cosign verify-blob succeeds against every published bundle, with no private key in the system.
  • One inventory, four reports, on every deploy, as an observable pipeline step.
  • Differentiated-status SSP generation: 115 hand-written hub controls, 218 resolved by family default, 333 total, all present in the published artifact.
  • Seven framework views from one Component Definition, each passing the NIST OSCAL schemas and fully determined after every beyond-Moderate control was dispositioned.
  • A customer-side local assessor that emits OSCAL Assessment Results validating against the NIST schema.
  • Runtime and deploy policy parity through identical compiled Wasm bytes.
  • Build-as-report for vulnerabilities: per-deploy emission with the Class C SLA enforced and blocking conditions wired.

One limit is structural and worth stating plainly here because it bounds every claim above: this is a single-producer demonstration. Cross-provider joins, portfolio aggregation, and programmatic third-party-risk consumption all require other parties to publish conformant signals. The schema makes those joins correct in principle; a working federation needs conformant signals that do not yet exist. The technical barrier is removed; the adoption barrier is not.

9. Threats to Validity

The assets are the published artifacts and the canonical inventory they share, which a consumer relies on to be authentic and current. The trust assumptions are explicit and external: GitHub's OIDC issuer for workflow identity, Fulcio for certificate issuance, Rekor for the transparency log, AWS KMS for the runtime signing key, and AWS for the runtime substrate. The signing chain removes per-system key custody on the deploy path and inherits the integrity of those services.

The adversary of interest would publish a false-but-signed artifact or silently diverge the live system from its description. The exact-identity pin defeats the first by construction: a signature from any workflow other than the named deploy workflow at the named ref fails verification, as the committed negative control confirms. Two residual surfaces remain, named rather than hidden. First, the deploy-time and runtime inputs pass through two hand-written adapters; the compiled policy is byte-identical across them but the adapters are not, so a divergence introduced in an adapter would not be caught by policy parity alone. Second, the runtime signal is signed with a KMS key rather than keyless, which trades the no-custody property of the deploy path for an AWS-managed key whose public half is published; a consumer's trust there rests on KMS rather than on Fulcio and Rekor. Neither surface is load-bearing for the deploy-time artifacts, but both are part of an honest account of the runtime drift detector.

Two further caveats bound interpretation. A valid signature attests provenance, not the truth of the claims it covers, so the schema validators and reconciliation gate, not the signature, are what make the contents trustworthy. And the “public by default” posture is an editorial choice that holds only for this system's threat model: a single operator, no federal data, no downstream tenants relying on its controls, artifacts public by design. A system whose compliance state is sensitive would gate it, and even here the IAM policy bodies are carried only as SHA-256 hashes, conceding that some inventory content is not for publication. The CR26 rule identifiers cited throughout are from the published final corpus (the Consolidated Rules for 2026, launched 2026-06-24).

10. Related Work

The control-authoring side has prior art. OpenControl and Compliance Masonry established treating control narratives as composable source; ComplianceAsCode/SSG generates hardening content and SSP fragments from machine-readable rules; and the OSCAL project itself defines the component-definition and profile-resolution model this work builds on directly. What is added here is the projection step measured end to end: one authored hub, read through profiles and crosswalks into many framework views.

The crosswalk side is where the caution lives. NIST's OLIR program publishes control-to-control mappings and is explicit that they are informational and not transitive, the exact property the CMMC section demonstrates on a live pipeline. The Secure Controls Framework and the CSA Cloud Controls Matrix are large hand-maintained crosswalks pursuing the same map-once-reuse-many goal at the catalog level rather than per system. On the provenance side, the work draws on a maturing stack, Sigstore (Fulcio, Rekor) for keyless signing and transparency, SLSA and in-toto for build provenance, OpenVEX for exploitability assertions, GUAC for supply-chain aggregation, and the CycloneDX Transparency Exchange API and IETF SCITT for standardizing how such attestations are exchanged; the canonical-inventory schema here is a small, opinionated instance of the identifier layer those efforts assume. On the policy side, GovRAMP's verify-once-use-many goal, the cATO literature around DoDI 8510.01, NISTIR 8011's automation of control assessment, and the FedRAMP 20x Phase One pilot all aim at continuous machine-readable authorization; this work is one self-contained instance of that direction, distinguished by being verifiable end to end at a stable URL rather than described.

11. Alignment with the Consolidated Rules for 2026

CR26 reorganizes the framework around machine-readable certification data, text-based equivalents in place of Word and Excel deliverables, and a set of rule families folded into the default requirements. This implementation mechanizes the families below and cites the rule IDs it implements; the IDs are from the published final corpus, the Consolidated Rules for 2026, launched 2026-06-24 and in effect for widespread adoption 2026-07-04 (active 20x certifications must conform by 2027-01-01).

  • MAS, Minimum Assessment Scope. Every component declares a FIPS 199 security category and information flows per MAS-CSO-FLO, with information resources identified per MAS-CSO-IIR and third-party AWS resources documented at the inheritance level per MAS-CSO-TPR. The schema enforces that the fields exist and are populated. MAS-CSO-MDI (federal-customer-data metadata) is not applicable here, because its MAS-CSO-IIR trigger does not fire with no federal customer data; MAS-CSO-SUP (supplemental, non-offering materials) carries nothing for this system.
  • SCN, Significant Change Notifications. CI validates every push and PR description for an SCN-Type: tag with routine-recurring as the default per SCN-RTR-NNR, and the git history is the audit record per SCN-CSO-MAR. Adaptive changes notify within 10 business days (SCN-ADP-NTF); Transformative changes carry five windows — 30 and 10 business days before starting (SCN-TRF-NIP/NFP), 5 after finishing and 5 after verification completes (SCN-TRF-NAF/NAV), and a 30-business-day service-documentation update (SCN-TRF-UPD) — with an optional recognized-assessor review (SCN-TRF-TPR).
  • CCM, Collaborative Continuous Monitoring. CR26 CCM is a quarterly cycle, not a stream. A quarterly human-readable Ongoing Certification Report (CCM-OCR-AVL) is regenerated on every deploy with the eight required summaries and an incident attestation, and the next-report and next-review target dates (CCM-OCR-NRD / CCM-QTR-NRD) are published with it, all Sigstore-signed and verifiable from anywhere. The four monitoring mechanisms feed it. The relationship-dependent rules — the synchronous Quarterly Review (CCM-QTR-MTG), the feedback channel (CCM-OCR-FBM), and the feedback summary (CCM-OCR-AFS) — are N/A absent a consuming agency. The strategy lives in docs/continuous-monitoring-plan.md.
  • VDR, Vulnerability Detection and Response. The build is the report. The aggregator classifies findings per VER-EVA-EPA (PAIN N1-N5), VER-EVA-EIR (internet-reachable), and VER-EVA-ELX (likely-exploitable), checks the CISA KEV catalog per VDR-TFR-KEV, and blocks when any finding exceeds the Class C tolerance in VDR-TFR-PVR. Per-deploy emission satisfies VER-RPT-PER and overshoots the monthly minimum in VER-TFR-MHR, which is published in both the signed JSON (VER-RPT-VDT's per-vulnerability record) and a human-readable Markdown rendering at /.well-known/vdr-report.md.
  • CDS, Certification Data Sharing. All certification data is published at /.well-known/ with no gating. The mandatory surface is produced as signed artifacts: a fifteen-field certification-overview package (CDS-CSO-PUB) carrying a FedRAMP-ID placeholder until one is assigned (CDS-CSO-FID) and a per-service list with security categories (CDS-CSO-SVC); a machine- and human-readable availability feed with a rolling 30-day window (CDS-CSO-AVR); and a machine-readable policy-and-procedure index (CDS-CSO-IRP). Each carries a JSON source of truth and a human-readable rendering kept consistent by the build (CDS-CSO-CBF), so a consumer with curl, jq, and cosign verifies the system's compliance state independently from anywhere. Two residuals are stated honestly rather than hidden: the availability feed shares the offering's CDN, so the “available even when the offering is down” clause of CDS-CSO-AVR is only partially met; and dated OCR-aligned package snapshots (CDS-CSO-HAD) are not yet retained, though the per-deploy artifacts and the availability and vulnerability trends provide rolling history. The trust-center subset (CDS-TRC-*, CDS-UTC-*) is N/A absent a consuming agency.
  • SDR, Security Decision Record. CR26 replaces the System Security Plan with a persistently maintained Security Decision Record (SDR-CSO-FRR): for each in-scope KSI, an explanation, the verification and validation evidence, and the rule-specific artifacts, with version and source metadata (SDR-CSO-MTD), per-KSI summaries (SDR-CSX-KSI), and metric history over 30 days and up to a year (SDR-CSX-KMT). It is derived from the same control hub as the OSCAL SSP and bound to the same inventory, so the two never disagree. Independent verification and validation are recorded as N/A — no FedRAMP Recognized assessor — rather than fabricated. Crucially, the OSCAL Rev 5 SSP is still generated: the SDR is the 20x-paradigm artifact and the SSP is the Rev 5-paradigm artifact, both projected from one inventory. The automation is paradigm-agnostic; the claim that one needs the lighter 20x path to do this is a strawman.
  • CPO, Certification Package Overview. The companion to the SDR (CPO-CSO-OVR): a human- and machine-readable overview that rolls up the CDS public information, the Minimum Assessment Scope, the cryptographic-module use (CMU-CSO-CMD), and the independent-assessment status (IVV-CSO-ICP, recorded N/A), names the accountable official (CPO-CSO-MTD), and indexes every signed artifact in the package. With the SDR it is the CR26 replacement for the base SSP; the Class C two-week maintenance cadence (CPO-CSX-CPM) is met trivially by the per-deploy pipeline.

CR26 also retires DOCX and XLSX for Class A through C certifications in favor of text-based equivalents (NTC-0009). Every artifact here is JSON, markdown, CSV, or YAML, all diff-able: the IIW CSV is the text-based equivalent of the Excel workbook, the OSCAL SSP carries the structured inventory it projects from, and the family policies, POA&M, monitoring plan, and Rules of Behavior are markdown. Each item in this section is something CR26 will require, mechanized ahead of the deadline rather than after it.

One pipeline, both paradigms. CR26 publishes two certification types, Rev 5 and 20x, and the final rules give them the same families: Minimum Assessment Scope, Significant Change Notifications, vulnerability detection and reporting, continuous monitoring, certification data sharing, cryptographic-module use, the Security Decision Record, and the Certification Package Overview all apply to both. The only material difference is the shape of the evidence: the Rev 5 Security Decision Record summarizes each applicable 800-53 control (SDR-CSF-CTF), while the 20x record summarizes each Key Security Indicator (SDR-CSX-KSI). This system produces both from the one control hub — the OSCAL Rev 5 SSP is the per-control evidence the Rev 5 record needs, and the KSI signal plus the 20x SDR are the per-indicator evidence the 20x record needs — and nothing in the automation cares which paradigm it is feeding. 20x is a lighter package to assemble, and that accessibility is real and worth having, but it is not what makes continuous, machine-checkable compliance possible. The same canonical inventory, the same reconciliation gate, and the same signing chain produce conformant evidence under either regime. The claim that an organization must wait for the lighter path to begin is a strawman: the heavier 800-53 paradigm is mechanized here too, from the same source.

12. Discussion: Where the Pattern Generalizes

Generalized, the technical pattern speaks to several recurring problems in cloud compliance, each a consequence of the same root. Assessment cost is dominated by reconciling the SSP against the live system rather than by evaluating control implementations; generating the SSP from the inventory closes that gap by construction (§6), which is what turns continuous assessment, the thing cATO promises and 20x is built around, from a theoretical goal into a tractable one. SSP drift, the most common audit finding, cannot arise between an SSP and a system that regenerates it on every deploy. Framework transition, the Rev 5 / 20x / state-program / CMMC / SOC 2 juggling that providers face in 2026, is exactly the hub-and-spoke case, and the seven live views are the proof: one program with several reports coming off the end rather than several programs. The same shape underlies cyber-insurance underwriting and vendor-risk management, both of which fail today for lack of current, trustworthy, machine-queryable data about the insured or supplied system; a continuously published, signed inventory removes that specific barrier, though whether insurers and TPRM functions adopt it is a market question rather than a technical one.

The pattern is not even specific to compliance. Wherever risk must be aggregated across heterogeneous systems run by independent parties, the aggregator's work is bounded by reconciliation cost rather than analytic insight, because every reporting entity uses different identifiers and schemas. Public-health surveillance is the sharpest example: an outbreak signal has to be assembled from hospitals, laboratories, and jurisdictions that each name patients, specimens, and locations their own way, so assembly swamps analysis and slows response. The canonical-inventory answer is the same everywhere: do the standardization at the layer below where the differentiation actually matters. The components for that layer already exist, PURL, Sigstore, CycloneDX HBOM, SLSA; what is missing is the will to assemble them and the agreement on where the standardization should live.

That last part is the hard part, and it is a coordination problem, not an engineering one. Providers compete partly on inventory opacity; compliance intermediaries earn the gap a canonical inventory would close; regulators have sunk costs in current frameworks. Each is a rational local incentive pulling against the global optimum, and coordination protocols that must overcome such incentives are slow: TCP/IP took roughly a decade to dominance, containerized shipping took thirty years, and both won on patient work plus early adopters who tolerated the cost of going first rather than on being obviously correct.

13. Conclusion

The cost commonly blamed on a lack of reciprocity is mostly the absence of the architectural reciprocity, the part that can be built without anyone's permission. This implementation builds it: one authored hub, seven framework views, a customer-side assessor, all derived on every deploy, signed, and verifiable from a stable URL for roughly $60 a year and a few weekends of engineering. The authorization reciprocity, the government decision, stays where it belongs and at the speed it deserves. The open question is not whether the evidence layer can exist; it is running and checkable. The question is whether enough producers build it for the multi-party payoff to arrive, which is a matter of coordination rather than schema. The technical answer already exists.
