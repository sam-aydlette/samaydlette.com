The Many Uses of an SBOM

The views and opinions expressed in this article are those of the author and do not reflect the views of any organization or employer.

What's an SBOM Anyway?

Let's start with the basics. A Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) is basically an ingredients list for your software. It's just like the nutrition label on your favorite snack, except instead of telling you there's too much sugar, it tells you there's too much Log4j. It's a formal, machine-readable inventory that identifies and lists all components in a piece of software. Importantly, it includes not only software vendors but also open-source packages, third-party libraries, and other dependencies.

Here's what a simple SBOM might look like in CycloneDX format (one of the most common standards):

{ "bomFormat": "CycloneDX", "specVersion": "1.4", "serialNumber": "urn:uuid:3e671687-395b-41f5-a30f-a58921a69b79", "version": 1, "components": [ { "type": "library", "name": "acme-library", "version": "1.0.0", "purl": "pkg:npm/acme-library@1.0.0" }, { "type": "framework", "name": "spring-core", "version": "5.3.19", "purl": "pkg:maven/org.springframework/spring-core@5.3.19" }, { "type": "library", "name": "log4j-core", "version": "2.17.1", "purl": "pkg:maven/org.apache.logging.log4j/log4j-core@2.17.1" } ] }

This SBOM reveals that your application uses three components: acme-library, spring-core, and log4j-core. If tomorrow a vulnerability is announced in log4j (again!?), you'd immediately know you're affected and exactly where to patch.

Why SBOMs Are Essential, Not Just Nice-to-Have

Software Bill Of Materials (SBOMs) are essential for modern software environments. While implementation challenges exist, the trajectory is clear: SBOMs are becoming a non-negotiable component of software security and regulatory compliance. Organizations that embrace them proactively will gain significant advantages in risk management, incident response, and customer trust. Here's why they matter to those of us building and securing cloud environments:

Visibility into Supply Chain, Distributed Components and Infrastructure as Code

In containerized and serverless environments, tracking what's actually running across your infrastructure can be difficult and expensive without proper automation and tooling. SBOMs give you instant visibility into every component across your Kubernetes clusters, serverless functions, and microservices. They help answer the question: "What's actually in production right now?"

Similarly, when your infrastructure is defined as code, SBOMs help you identify vulnerable dependencies in your Terraform modules, CloudFormation templates, or Pulumi stacks. By integrating SBOM generation into your IaC pipelines, you can catch security issues before they reach your cloud environment.

Those container images you're pulling from Docker Hub or ECR? An SBOM reveals exactly what's inside, including base images, installed packages, and applications. This allows organizations to analyze supply chain risk across every dependency.

This visibility is invaluable when investigating security events to quickly determine if suspicious behavior is related to vulnerable components and prioritize responses.

The integration of SBOMs into vulnerability management can also dramatically reduce alert fatigue. Teams can focus on actual exploitable vulnerabilities rather than wasting time determining whether tickets are findings versus false positives, making remediation efforts more efficient and targeted. When the next Log4Shell happens (and it will), having current SBOMs means you can immediately identify every affected resource.

Cloud Cost Optimization

Beyond security, SBOMs help identify redundant or outdated components consuming cloud resources. By eliminating unnecessary dependencies, you can reduce container sizes, decrease memory footprints, and ultimately lower your monthly cloud bill.

SBOMs also provide critical visibility into licensing requirements for open source components, helping organizations track compliance with license terms. This visibility reduces legal risk by ensuring proper attribution, identifying components with restrictive licenses, and preventing unintentional violations that could lead to costly litigation or unplanned licensing costs

SBOM generation fits into existing CI/CD approaches. With free, open source tools like Grype, you can automatically generate SBOMs during container builds and gate deployments based on security findings without adding any friction or undue cost.

Security Metrics That Matter

SBOMs enable near real-time security metrics and insights that resonate with executive leadership. Some ideas for reporting include Mean Time To Remediate (MTTR), the percentage of late critical findings, or the percentage of findings per engineering team, component, or vendor. These metrics help justify security investments and demonstrate the effectiveness of a security program far beyond traditional compliance checkboxes.

Getting Started with SBOMs: A Practical Guide

Convinced you need SBOMs but not sure where to start? Here's a practical approach to implement SBOMs in your organization:

Step 1: Choose Your Tools

Several excellent open-source tools can help generate SBOMs at different points in your software lifecycle:

  • Syft Syft is an open-source CLI tool developed by Anchore that scans container images and file systems to generate comprehensive Software Bills of Materials (SBOMs) in multiple formats (CycloneDX, SPDX), identifying packages and dependencies regardless of ecosystem or language to provide visibility into software components and supply chain security.
  • Grype - Open source container vulnerability scanner (from the same team as Syft) that works directly with Syft's SBOMs to identify vulnerabilities
  • CycloneDX - CycloneDX is an open-source, lightweight SBOM standard that uses XML or JSON format to track software components, their dependencies, and their attributes throughout the software supply chain, and supports multiple languages including Java, .NET, Python, and more
  • SPDX SBOM Generator - Creates SPDX-format SBOMs (ISO/IEC 5962:2021 standard)
  • Dependency-Track - Dependency-Track is an open-source, component vulnerability management platform that ingests and analyzes Software Bills of Materials (SBOMs), continuously monitors them for known vulnerabilities in their components, provides risk scoring, and helps organizations track and manage their software supply chain security across their entire application portfolio. It essentially serves as a central repository and analysis tool for your SBOMs after they've been generated.
  • ORT (OSS Review Toolkit) - ORT (OSS Review Toolkit) is a comprehensive open-source suite of tools that handles the entire workflow for analyzing software dependencies, including automatically scanning projects for their dependencies, checking license compliance, detecting vulnerabilities, and generating standardized SBOMs for open source components across multiple package ecosystems and languages.

Step 2: Integrate with Your CI/CD Pipeline

SBOM generation should be automated as part of your build process. Here's how to integrate with common CI/CD platforms:

# GitHub Actions Example name: Generate SBOM on: push: branches: [ main ] jobs: generate-sbom: runs-on: ubuntu-latest steps: - uses: actions/checkout@v3 - name: Generate SBOM uses: anchore/sbom-action@v0 with: format: cyclonedx-json output-file: sbom.json - name: Upload SBOM uses: actions/upload-artifact@v3 with: name: sbom path: sbom.json

For Jenkins, you can use a pipeline script that calls the same tools:

pipeline { agent any stages { stage('Generate SBOM') { steps { sh 'syft packages alpine:latest -o cyclonedx-json > sbom.json' archiveArtifacts artifacts: 'sbom.json', fingerprint: true } } } }

Common Challenges and How to Overcome Them

Let's be real - implementing SBOMs isn't all trivial. While the benefits are substantial, organizations typically face several hurdles along the way. Large environments with thousands of containers or applications can generate overwhelming volumes of SBOM data, creating significant scalability challenges. Rather than avoiding SBOMs altogether, successful implementations focus on proper data management through deduplication (avoiding storage of identical components multiple times), structured databases instead of flat files, hierarchical SBOMs that allow for component reuse, and selective filtering to prioritize components with known vulnerabilities or specific licenses.

Accuracy issues present another common obstacle, as no SBOM generator is perfect. Missing or misidentified components can create both false negatives and false positives that undermine trust in the system. Package URL (PURL) specifications play a crucial role in mitigating these challenges by providing a standardized, unambiguous way to identify components across different ecosystems. By implementing PURL in your SBOM workflow, you dramatically reduce identification errors and improve cross-referencing accuracy with vulnerability databases. Beyond PURL adoption, organizations can address accuracy issues by employing multiple scanners and comparing results, implementing manual verification processes for mission-critical applications, establishing formal procedures for correcting and updating SBOMs when inaccuracies are identified, and specifically checking for nested dependencies that might be missed by surface-level scans.

Finally, SBOMs require robust governance to remain valuable over time. This includes establishing clear ownership for SBOM accuracy (typically shared between security and development teams), creating update policies that automatically trigger SBOM regeneration when dependencies change, defining retention policies for historical SBOMs to support incident investigation, and implementing cryptographic attestation mechanisms to verify SBOM integrity and authenticity throughout their lifecycle.

Hypothetical Case Study: SBOMs in Action During a Security Incident

Imagine a mid-sized financial services company that recently implemented an SBOM program across their cloud environment. Several months after implementation, their security team discovers suspicious activity in their production environment. Security logs show unusual API calls originating from a customer data processing microservice, raising immediate concerns. Without clear indicators pointing to the specific vulnerability, the team could be facing days of investigation and potential data exposure.

However, with their SBOM system in place, they're able to quickly query their component inventory to identify everything running in the affected microservice. Cross-referencing these components against recently disclosed vulnerabilities immediately flags a third-party API client library using a vulnerable JSON parser with a remote code execution vulnerability. More importantly, they can instantly identify every other application using the same vulnerable component across their infrastructure.

Instead of the usual scramble of manually checking code repositories, the team immediately isolates the vulnerable services and deploys patched versions within hours of initial detection. What might have been a multi-day incident with potential regulatory implications becomes a controlled situation resolved within a single workday. The company's executives, previously skeptical about investing in an SBOM program, now see tangible value in their security investment.

Before and After: The Impact of SBOM Implementation

While every organization's results vary, our hypothetical financial services company experienced substantial improvements across several key metrics after fully implementing their SBOM program. The time required to identify affected systems after vulnerability disclosures dropped from days to hours. Their vulnerability remediation completion rate within SLA timeframes improved dramatically. False positives in vulnerability scanning decreased significantly, reducing wasted effort investigating non-issues. The personnel hours required per vulnerability response shrank substantially, and unplanned outages during emergency patching became rare events rather than common occurrences.

Beyond security improvements, the organization discovered unexpected benefits in cloud resource optimization. By identifying unused and redundant dependencies through their SBOM program, they were able to reduce container sizes and optimize runtime footprints, resulting in meaningful reductions in overall cloud costs. Perhaps most importantly, the security team transformed their reputation within the organization, moving from being seen as a roadblock to being recognized as enablers of secure, efficient operations.

The Bottom Line

SBOMs bridge the visibility gap between cloud infrastructure and security controls, enabling organizations to protect modern environments at the speed and scale of cloud development. Cloud security and engineering leaders need practical security solutions that work at scale without compromising the speed and flexibility that makes cloud computing powerful. SBOMs provide exactly that.
